Reservoir Sampling

Content Warning: python, algorithms, data science

In [1]:
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

What do you do when you want to take a random sample from a data stream where the size of that stream is unknown or will never end? Sampling is used for all manner of inference and analytic tools and it certainly seems like a useful thing to do, but the standard approach to taking random samples (sample each element with $1/n$ probability where $n$ is the number of items in your set) fails when you have a stream like this.

Plenty of folks have written about this problem and its variants, but here is my take on it and a little demonstration for the non-believers.

Formally, we can define the problem as:

  • How do we sample $k$ elements from a stream $X$ with length $n >> k$?

The basic approach here is to make a reservoir to hold the $k$ elements and replace items in the reservior with certain probabilities.

Let $X = \{x_1, x_2, ..., x_i\}$. First, keep all elements in the reservoir up to size $k$. This is your base.

For every remaining $x \in X$, we decide to keep $x_i$ with probability $\frac{1}{n}$, where $n$ is the number of items we've encountered from $X$ thus far. Since we only replace elements in the reservoir when we choose to keep $x_i$, any item in the reservoir has an $\frac{n-1}{n}$ probability of being kept.

This gives us an overall probability of keeping $x_i$ of $$\frac{1}{n-1} \times \frac{n-1}{n}$$ $$= \frac{1}{n}$$

If we do keep $x_i$, we simply replace an item from the reservoir at random with $x_i$.

In python, the function might look something like this:

In [2]:
def reservoir_sample(stream, size):
    """
    maintains a random sample from a stream.
    Args:
        stream: iterable item
        size: size of the sample you want
    """
    reservoir = []    
    for i, x in enumerate(stream, start=1):
        if i <= size:
            reservoir.append(x)
        elif random.random() < (1 / i):
            reservoir[random.randint(0, size-1)] = x
    return reservoir

Python's nifty generators make defining streams easy. Let's test our algorithm over two distributions, gaussian and uniform, to see if the resulting samples are representative of the streams.

I also took the opportunity to test Anaconda's numba just-in-time compiler, which handily is invoked with a decorator.

In [7]:
@jit(nopython=True)
def gauss(size, mean=100, std=15):
    """Gives a gaussian distributed stream with definable mean and std.
    Args:
        size: size of the stream
        mean: mean of the gaussian distribution
        std: standard devaiation of the gaussian dist
    """
    # return numbers with mean = 100, std = 15
    while size:
        size-= 1
        yield int(random.gauss(mean, std))

@jit(nopython=True)
def unif(size, lower=50, upper=150):
    """Gives a uniform distributed stream with definable range
    Args:
        size: size of the stream
        low: lower bound of the uniform range
        high: upper bound of the uniform range
    """
    # keep the range inclusive of upper bound
    upper += 1
    while size:
        size-= 1
        yield random.randrange(lower, upper)
